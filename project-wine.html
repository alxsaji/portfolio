Context

This project emerged from a critical challenge in inventory management for wine distribution. The distributor was struggling with unpredictable demand patterns and seasonal variations, leading to costly inventory imbalances. Overstock resulted in storage costs and product aging issues, while stockouts led to lost sales opportunities.

The challenge stems from the complexity of demand forecasting in the wine industry, where seasonal patterns, trends, and external factors all influence sales. Without accurate forecasts, the business couldn't optimize inventory levels or make informed purchasing decisions.

Dataset Overview

The analysis utilized monthly wine sales data containing 187 time periods from January 1980 to July 1995, with 185 non-null observations (2 missing values). The dataset included:

  • Monthly sales volumes for Rose wine
  • Time series data spanning over 15 years
  • Seasonal patterns and trends in wine consumption
  • Cyclical variations in sales patterns

The time series data provided a comprehensive view of wine sales patterns, enabling analysis of trends, seasonality, and cyclical behaviors essential for accurate forecasting using ARIMA and SARIMA models.

Problem

There is an abundance of historical sales data but lack of reliable forecasting methods for operations teams to determine future demand patterns and optimize inventory levels and purchasing decisions.

How Might We

"How might we develop accurate sales forecasts to optimize inventory levels, reduce stockouts and overstock, and improve cash flow management in wine distribution?"

Operations teams face several key challenges:

  • Difficulty predicting seasonal demand variations
  • Lack of understanding of underlying sales trends
  • Inability to account for cyclical patterns in forecasting
  • Limited visibility into future demand uncertainty
  • Challenges in balancing inventory costs with service levels

Project Goal

Make the process of sales forecasting accurate, reliable, and actionable for the wine distributor, enabling them to optimize inventory levels, reduce costs, and improve operational efficiency.

Solution

"Forecast demand with accuracy, precision, and confidence – the goal is to Optimize Inventory Management."

Developed a comprehensive time series forecasting solution using advanced statistical models including ARIMA and SARIMA. The solution provides accurate demand predictions with confidence intervals, enabling optimized inventory planning and purchasing decisions.

Approach

The project was developed using a systematic time series forecasting methodology, following best practices in statistical modeling, model selection, and forecast validation. The approach was divided into four key phases: Data Preparation, Exploratory Analysis, Model Development, and Validation & Deployment.

Phase 1

Data Collection & Preparation

"What historical data do we need, and how do we prepare it for time series analysis?"

Collected monthly wine sales data from CSV files containing 187 time periods from January 1980 to July 1995. Performed data cleaning including handling 2 missing values (July and August 1994), converting date formats using datetime parsing, and validating data integrity. Created time series objects with proper date indexing (DatetimeIndex). Ensured data quality and consistency for time series modeling by checking for null values and data types.

Phase 2

Exploratory Time Series Analysis

"What patterns and characteristics exist in the sales data?"

Performed comprehensive exploratory time series analysis to identify trends, seasonality, and patterns. Conducted time series decomposition using seasonal_decompose to separate trend, seasonal, and residual components. Applied stationarity tests using Augmented Dickey-Fuller (ADF) test to determine if differencing was needed. Analyzed autocorrelation (ACF) and partial autocorrelation (PACF) plots using plot_acf and plot_pacf to understand data structure and identify appropriate ARIMA model parameters. Created month plots to visualize seasonal patterns.

Phase 3

Model Development & Optimization

"Which models provide the best forecast accuracy?"

Applied differencing to achieve stationarity where needed based on ADF test results. Built ARIMA models using statsmodels.tsa.arima_model.ARIMA and optimized parameters (p, d, q) using ACF and PACF plots. Developed SARIMA models to capture seasonal patterns with seasonal parameters (P, D, Q, s) where s=12 for monthly data. Tested multiple model configurations including Exponential Smoothing, Simple Exponential Smoothing, and Holt's method. Selected optimal parameters using AIC and BIC criteria. Compared model performance to identify the best forecasting approach.

Phase 4

Validation & Forecast Generation

"How do we validate model accuracy and generate reliable forecasts?"

Validated models using rolling window cross-validation and train-test splits to assess forecast accuracy. Evaluated model performance using comprehensive metrics including MAPE (Mean Absolute Percentage Error), RMSE (Root Mean Squared Error), and MAE (Mean Absolute Error) from sklearn.metrics. Generated multi-step ahead forecasts with confidence intervals. Conducted model diagnostics including residual analysis, Q-Q plots, and Ljung-Box tests to ensure forecast reliability. Visualized forecast results alongside historical data to assess model fit.

Model Comparison

Multiple forecasting models were evaluated and compared to identify the best approach for wine sales forecasting. The comparison includes Simple Moving Average, ARIMA, and other time series models.

Simple Moving Average Method

The Simple Moving Average method provides a baseline forecast by averaging historical values. While it smooths out fluctuations during the training period, it produces a flat forecast for future periods, which may not capture seasonal patterns effectively.

ARIMA Model Comparison

The ARIMA (AutoRegressive Integrated Moving Average) model demonstrates superior performance by capturing both trend and seasonal patterns. The model shows excellent fit on training data and provides accurate forecasts for the test period, closely tracking the actual observed values.

Final Forecast Model

The final selected model provides multi-step ahead forecasts with confidence intervals, enabling data-driven inventory planning and risk management for wine sales.

Forecast with Confidence Intervals

The final forecast model extends predictions into the future while maintaining the observed seasonal patterns. The confidence intervals (shaded area) represent forecast uncertainty, with wider intervals indicating greater uncertainty for longer prediction horizons.

Reflection

This wine sales forecasting project has been a rewarding experience that pushed me to think beyond basic statistical methods and explore the power of time series analysis in demand forecasting.

Time Series Analysis Requires Careful Exploration

I learned that successful time series forecasting requires thorough exploratory analysis. Understanding the data's characteristics - trends, seasonality, and stationarity - is crucial before selecting and building models. The time spent on exploratory analysis paid off significantly in model accuracy.

Model Selection is Both Art and Science

I discovered that model selection involves balancing statistical criteria (AIC, BIC) with business understanding. The SARIMA model captured seasonal patterns that ARIMA couldn't, demonstrating the importance of choosing models that match the data's characteristics. However, more complex models aren't always better - simplicity and interpretability matter too.

Forecast Uncertainty is Valuable

Providing confidence intervals alongside point forecasts was crucial for risk management. The business could make informed decisions about inventory levels by understanding forecast uncertainty. This experience taught me that communicating uncertainty is as important as the forecast itself.
